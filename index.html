About

Partha Pratim Majumder, renowned for his contributions to genetics and statistics. Best known in the media as “Gene Guru,” one of his main interests is to understand the evolution of contemporary human populations, and he has worked hard to uncover the genetic structure that underlies disease or drug susceptibility. Applied statistical research and algorithm development was another part of his passion and brilliant genius. He has own numerous national and international awards, published hundreds of scholarly research findings and led multiple big consortium projects in India and abroad.

He obtained his Bachelor’s, Master’s and Doctoral degrees from the Indian Statistical Institute, Kolkata. He did his post-doctoral research at the Center for Demographic & Population Genetics, University of Texas Medical Center, Houston. He has served for two years, 1987-1989, as visiting assistant professor at the University of Pittsburgh before returning home and joining his alma mater ISI's Anthropology and Human Genetics Unit. Later he founded two institutes – one in Kolkata, Center for Population Genomics and second one in Kalyani, National Institute of Biomedical Genomics.

Facts

The transition from statistics training to genetics research was a matter of course for him. He began his research in population genetics. While many are incapable of multitasking, he was a PhD student and a postdoctoral fellow at the same time!

CONTRIBUTIONS:

Professor Majumder has made extra-ordinary contributions to the understanding of human genetics and evolution using statistical, genomic and anthropological insights. He has developed innovative paradigms and new statistical methods to solve biological problems related to modes of inheritance of complex human traits and mapping genes that modulates these traits. He used new statistical algorithms to analyze demographic and genomic data on various common human disorders and their quantitative precursor states, that has resulted in deeper understanding of the genetic bases of such disorders. He was one of the early human geneticists to recognize the significance of studying genetic structures of ethnic populations using genomic assays to discover genes conferring susceptibilities to various common diseases. This viewpoint has been widely accepted later and helped the researchers to study the genetics of common diseases in the villages of population isolates. His research studies on genomic diversity of ethnic Indian populations has delineated the early footsteps of the peopling of the Indian subcontinent. This baseline understanding of the human genetic structure have had major contributions to the design of studies for mapping disease genes. Prof Majumder's group has following a cohort of 20,000 volunteers in and around the city of Kalyani in West Bengal for last 10 years to understand human health trajectories over a period of time. He wanted to find changes in health-related parameters that are best explained by inter-individual differences in participant’s genomes. He made important contributions to the understanding of differential response to vaccinated individuals of typhoid and cholera by looking into fine-scale map of human genetic variation. He led the International Cancer Genome Consoritum project – India component – on oral cancer to find out the genomic, transcriptomic and epi-genomic drivers of oral cancer pathogenesis, recurrence and metastasis. He has guided and taught large number of individuals in the field of human genomic research who would continue to transfer his vast knowledgebase to the next generation budding scientists.
